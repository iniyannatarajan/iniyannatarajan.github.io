Do Not Go Gentle Into That Good Night

Ever since Albert Einstein published the General Theory of Relativity in 1915, black holes have been a mathematical possibility that remained firmly in the domain of science fiction for decades. Indirect evidence for their existence started accumulating in the last few decades, although black holes themselves eluded direct observation. Then, in relatively quick succession, two historic scientific results were published that proved beyond a shadow of doubt that black holes are stuff of reality. In 2016, for the first time, LIGO detected gravitational waves emitted by two merging black holes;and in 2019, the Event Horizon Telescope Consortium unveiled the historic first image of a supermassive black hole at the heart of the giant elliptical galaxy M87 (designated M87*) located about 53.5 million light-years (the distance that light travels in one year) away.

The event horizon of a black hole is the radius around the singularity (where matter has collapsed to infinite density) at which matter and energy cannot escape a black hole’s gravity and are doomed to disappear forever. How do we photograph something from which not even light can escape? Fortunately (for someone located at a safe distance!),a black hole feeds on stars, gas,and dust, which form a superheated flattened accretion disc around it and jets of matter travelling at speeds comparable to that of light from its polar regions. The paths of the photons emitted from the disc and the jets are bent by the strong gravity just outside the event horizon, forming a bright ring around a dark shadow, with the photons travelling towards us appearing brighter than the ones travelling away from us.

Supermassive black holes can be enormous, dwarfing entire solar systems, but they are also located at large distances, making them incredibly tiny in the night sky. The apparent diameter of M87* on the sky is 46.5 million times smaller than that of the Moon! To resolve an object this tiny, observing ataradio wavelength of 1.3 mm at which the centre of M87 becomes visible, we need a telescope the size of the Earth. To achieve this, we harness the power of very long baseline interferometry (VLBI), the technique of operating multiple telescopes around the world together in such a way that a large telescope is synthesised. The Event Horizon Telescope (EHT) is one such global network of radio telescopes that creates an Earth-sized telescope, which trained its eyes towards the centre of M87 for four nights in April 2017. The signals arriving at the individual telescopes were recorded on hundreds of hard disks and were flown to central facilities which combined these data and measured the strength and shape of the weak signals hiding in them in a process known as calibration. The next challenge was to create images from the calibrated data.

Scientists are normal people with beliefs and prejudices that influence their inferences. One often sees what one wants to see! To combat these biases, four teams worked independently on creating images from the data, with different algorithms and assumptions going into the imaging process. In the second stage, three different imaging software were used to create images from both actual and synthetic data generated based on theoretical models of black holes and realistic weather conditions at the telescope sites during actual observations. In both stages, the dominant feature in the reconstructed images was found to be an asymmetric photon ring with a diameter consistent with the shadow of a supermassive black hole with a mass of 6.5 billion Suns. Extensive statistical analyses of the data before and after imaging also corroborated these results.

The planned next-generation EHT (ngEHT) will add more telescopes to the existing array, which will help us make sharper images of these black holes. Algorithms for making black hole movies to capture the movement of matter and radiation around them are being developed. Development of these capabilities is a necessary step in the quest for imaging the fast-rotating supermassive black hole in the nucleus of the Milky Way, known as Sagittarius A*. A more ambitious expansion of the EHT into space will bring other interesting objects within the resolvable range and reveal the intricate structure of the infinite subrings in the unresolved photon ring of a black hole. The first M87* images released last year were only the beginning.
