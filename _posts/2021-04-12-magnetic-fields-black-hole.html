On March 24, 2021, the Event Horizon Telescope Collaboration released new images of the supermassive black hole at the heart of the M87 galaxy, this time in polarised light. This work is a culmination of two years of intense polarimetric analysis of the data gathered in 2017 by the Collaboration.

So, what do we see in this new image, and why is it important? Let's dive right in.

Electromagnetism and polarisation

We see waves rippling on the surface of water when a stone is thrown. We hear when sound waves are generated as a result of disturbances in the air. Such waves are known as mechanical waves. Mechanical waves cannot travel through vacuum and require a medium to propagate in. Mechanical waves transport energy (not matter - a piece of paper lying on the surface of water is not carried outwards by the wave; it just bobs up and down) through the medium.

In contrast, electromagnetic waves do not require a medium to travel. It has been known for well over a century that changing electric fields generate magnetic fields and vice versa. These dynamically changing fields form electromagnetic fields. Once these changing fields have been set in motion, the wave takes on a life of its own and does not require a medium to travel in. This is why, in the famous docking scene in the movie Interstellar, we see the explosion, but do not hear it (kudos to the makers for respecting science)! The frequency of oscillation determines the properties of the electromagnetic wave. Radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, and gamma rays are all electromagnetic waves, that differ only in frequency.

Physics tells us that the changing electric and magnetic fields are oriented perpendicular to each other, with the electromagnetic wave travelling in a direction perpendicular to them both. In unpolarised light, the electric fields can be oriented along any direction. The electric fields of the waves are oriented randomly with respect to each other. When light is polarised, the electric fields of all the waves that reach our eye are preferentially pointing (or rotating, but we will ignore this for our present purposes!) in one direction.
