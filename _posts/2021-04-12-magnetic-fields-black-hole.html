On March 24, 2021, the Event Horizon Telescope Collaboration released new images of the supermassive black hole at the heart of the M87 galaxy, this time in polarised light. This work is a culmination of two years of intense polarimetric analysis of the data gathered in 2017 by the Collaboration.

So, what do we see in this new image, and why is it important? Let's dive right in.

What is polarisation?

We have all seen waves rippling on the surface of water when a stone is thrown. We know that sound waves are generated as a result of disturbances in the air. Such waves are known as mechanical waves. Mechanical waves cannot travel through vacuum and require a medium to propagate in. Mechanical waves transport energy (not matter; a piece of paper lying on the surface of water is not carried outwards by the wave) through the medium.

In contrast, electromagnetic waves are formed in a completely different way. It has been known for well over a century that changing electric fields generate magnetic fields, and changing magnetic fields generate electric fields. These dynamically changing fields form electromagnetic waves. Once these changing fields have been set in motion, the wave takes on a life of its own and does not require a medium to travel in. This is why, in the famous docking scene in the movie Interstellar, we see the explosion, but do not hear it (kudos to the makers for respecting science)!
