The Writing Process

How often should you write? There is no one answer that fits everyone. You can either adhere to a strict schedule and force yourself to write everyday, or write in irregular bursts. What is not adivsable, is to try and write an entire thesis in the last few weeks of a Masters or a PhD. Even if your writing skills are top-notch, starting to write early is necessary to develop a vision for the project and avoid unpleasant surprises. More often than not, new analyses will have to be performed and some old ones repeated. Life may interfere in various ways, ranging from personal issues to global pandemics! At such times, it is always heartening to have parts of the thesis ready, not least to remind yourself of what you are capable of and that half the work has already been done.

Another reason to start writing early is that it helps to iterate and improve. Good friends and colleagues, even (or rather, especially) those who do not work in the same field, can serve as great sounding boards. Thesis advisors or project PIs and mentors (if you are lucky enough to have a good one) are natural choices when it comes to those within the field. A large number of corrections is not necessarily an indictment on your writing skills; it is best thought of as well-meaning suggestions for improvement from people who took the time to read your writing (although not always true, this is what it exactly is most of the time). And when different people give different suggestions, the best solution is to synthesize the optimal way to convey the same ideas better, for which the diverging suggestions can provide a foundation. From experience, it often happens that thesis advisors revert the changes they originally suggested in a previous iteration, but this is usually not done out of spite. It just means that there is more than one good way of imparting the same information or that the merits of the previous approach became clearer only after the alternative was tried.

Finally, I want to briefly touch on writer's block, which warrants its own post (which I may find the time to write up at some point in the future). Theses and papers do not come out fully formed and, when faced with the prospect of not having any material written down versus having some bullet points and not very well-written sentences, it is better to choose the latter, since they can be improved in subsequent iterations. Writing is an iterative process and you should not hesitate to make it so. Regular stream of consciousness writing (of anything that comes to your mind) can help break this negative feedback cycle when sitting down to write.

For Exophonic Writers

For better or worse, academic journals with the most international readership are in English, and hence speakers of all languages end up having to write in English. This is not as much of an impediment as some might think or fear. Once someone has mastered the basics of any language, they will find that writing well in that language does not have anything to do with being a native speaker of that language. You can always look up words in a dictionary or a thesaurus, but writing skills are not synonymous with knowledge of a particular language. I personally prefer having an online dictionary/thesaurus open while writing, along with the excellent Google Ngram viewer.

There are advantages to being an exophonic writer. New ways to conceptualise and express ideas are available without additional effort, and the farther ones home language is from English, the more novel these ways can be, and can add spice to ones writing. This, to some extent, offsets the disadvantage of not having the large vocabulary that a native speaker might have acquired over years. Also, when in doubt, you can form the sentence first in your home language to improve the flow. Many English writers famous for their beautiful prose are exophonic and hail from all over the world! Language is a tool, not your master!

Last but not least, once in a while, remember to flout any of the above rules and recommendations judiciously! Happy writing!
