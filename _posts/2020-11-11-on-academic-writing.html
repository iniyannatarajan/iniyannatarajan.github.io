While conversing is intuitive, writing is anything but. There is no interlocutor to complement the speaker, no live audience to react. As Charles Darwin wrote, "Man has an instinctive tendency to speak, as we see in the babble of our young children, whereas no child has an instinctive tendency to bake, brew, or write". Hence, the writer must develop (i) a clear perception of the fantasy world they inhabit, and (ii) the ability to inhabit the reader's shoes, to discover the best way to guide the reader to see what the writer already sees. This is especially true for academics, for whom bringing the fruits of their research to the rest of humanity is an important part of their work, although it is often performed almost as an afterthought. If a Justice of the Supreme Court, whose entire career was spent in dealing with legalese, could learn how to write from a prose stylist such as Nabokov, then so can academics!

Different purposes take centre stage in different situations in academic writing. Narration tells a story with an overarching structure.
