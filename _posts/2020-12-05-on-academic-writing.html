While conversing is intuitive, writing is anything but. There is no interlocutor to complement the speaker, no live audience to react. As Charles Darwin wrote, "Man has an instinctive tendency to speak, as we see in the babble of our young children, whereas no child has an instinctive tendency to bake, brew, or write". Hence, the writer must develop (i) a clear perception of the fantasy world they inhabit, and (ii) the ability to inhabit the reader's shoes, to discover the best way to guide the reader to see what the writer already sees. This is especially true for academics, for whom bringing the fruits of their research to the rest of humanity relies on their writing being clear and unambiguous, although it is often performed almost as an afterthought. If Ruth Bader Ginsburg, a Justice of the Supreme Court whose entire career had to be spent in dealing with legalese, could learn how to write from a prose stylist such as Nabokov, then so can academics!

Modes of Writing

The folks at WriteAtHome have produced a nice graphic that summarises four basic modes of writing - narration, description, exposition, and persuasion. Although research papers are classified under 'exposition', we can easily extend this to show that all four modes take centre stage at different situations in academic writing.

  • Narration tells a story with an overarching structure; dissertations and journal articles must ensure that the there is an overll narrative structure to their story.

  • Description gives an account of an object or an event and is the primary aim of chapters and sections that deal with experimental setup and methods.

  • Exposition is the process of explaining something in detail and review articles and chapters are expository in nature.

  • Persuasion is to argue in favour of a theory. This may be mildly surprising to some, but persuasion plays a large part in scientific writing, especially when discussing ones interpretation of their results and while writing observing or grant proposals. The aim here (ideally) is not to mislead the reader into believing something that is not true, but to argue clearly why a certain endeavour is worth undertaking despite its limitations.

The concept of style

Style, in this sense, is not the opposite of substance. Writing with style is (again, ideally) not about hiding bad research with misleading arguments; clear writing exposes the flaws in ones arguments, so that someone (including the writer themselves) can pick them up more easily.

Though we may not be aware of it, we all possess a default style acquired unconsciously. When an academic is called upon to write, it is this default style that they fall back on, which may not be good enough for the task at hand. The so-called classic style is specifically suited for such situations, especially for academics. Following Francis-Noël Thomas and Mark Turner in Clear and Simple as the Truth, we may assign the following characteristics to classic style:

  • Focuses on clarity and simplicity i.e., getting the message across.

  • Endeavours to make itself invisible i.e., the writing doesn't get in the way of the message.

  • Treats the reader as an equal.

  • Does not hesitate to adopt a conversational tone.
