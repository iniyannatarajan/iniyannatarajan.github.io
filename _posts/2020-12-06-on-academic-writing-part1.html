While conversing is intuitive, writing is anything but. There is no interlocutor to complement the speaker, no live audience to react. As Charles Darwin wrote, "Man has an instinctive tendency to speak, as we see in the babble of our young children, whereas no child has an instinctive tendency to bake, brew, or write". Hence, the writer must develop (i) a clear perception of the fantasy world they inhabit, and (ii) the ability to inhabit the reader's shoes, to discover the best way to guide the reader to see what the writer already sees. This is especially true for academics, for whom bringing the fruits of their research to the rest of humanity relies on their writing being clear and unambiguous, although it is often performed almost as an afterthought. If Ruth Bader Ginsburg, a Justice of the Supreme Court whose entire career had to be spent in dealing with legalese, could learn how to write from a prose stylist such as Nabokov, then so can academics!

Modes of Writing

The folks at WriteAtHome have produced a nice graphic that summarises four basic modes of writing - narration, description, exposition, and persuasion. Although research papers are classified under 'exposition', we can easily extend this to show that all four modes take centre stage at different situations in academic writing.

  • Narration tells a story with an overarching structure; dissertations and journal articles must ensure that the there is an overll narrative structure to their story.

  • Description gives an account of an object or an event and is the primary aim of chapters and sections that deal with experimental setup and methods.

  • Exposition is the process of explaining something in detail and review articles and chapters are expository in nature.

  • Persuasion is to argue in favour of a theory. This may be mildly surprising to some, but persuasion plays a large part in scientific writing, especially when discussing ones interpretation of their results and while writing observing or grant proposals. The aim here (ideally) is not to mislead the reader into believing something that is not true, but to argue clearly why a certain endeavour is worth undertaking despite its limitations.

The concept of style

Style, in this sense, is not the opposite of substance. Writing with style is (again, ideally) not about hiding bad research with misleading arguments; clear writing exposes the flaws in ones arguments, so that someone (including the writer themselves) can pick them up more easily.

Though we may not be aware of it, we all possess a default style acquired unconsciously. When an academic is called upon to write, it is this default style that they fall back on, which may not be good enough for the task at hand. The so-called classic style is specifically suited for such situations, especially for academics. Following Francis-Noël Thomas and Mark Turner in Clear and Simple as the Truth, we may assign the following characteristics to classic style:

  • Focuses on clarity and simplicity i.e., getting the message across.

  • Endeavours to make itself invisible i.e., the writing doesn't get in the way of the message.

  • Treats the reader as an equal.

  • Does not hesitate to adopt a conversational tone.

Let us dive deeper into some aspects of writing that are best avoided by academics.

(i) Using self-conscious, long-winded prose

Page count does not determine the quality of a dissertation or a thesis; and fancier words do not make a statement more precise. Great PhD theses can be fewer than a 100 pages long, written in simple, straightforward language. Contrary to what some academics believe, simplicity in writing is not easily achieved. The writer makes a conscious effort to hide all the effort so that the writing disappears into the background. Parsimony requires practice. It is worth exploring what makes academics use long-winded prose. Daniel Oppenheimer from Princeton University, who won the Ig Nobel prize for studying this phenomenon, had the following to say:

Tongue in cheek, he titled this article Consequences of erudite vernacular utilized irrespective of necessity: problems with using long words needlessly!

(ii) Obsession with passive voice

Another one of those near-ubiquitous recommendations that turns out to be wrong. This bias need not always be conscious. Academics who push for the passive consciously, have a vague idea of needing to be objective in presenting their research, and hiding the doer (i.e., the subject) and focusing on the object is mistakenly thought of as satisfying that need. In The Sense of Style, Steven Pinker explains why some people subconsciously prefer the passive. While good writers narrate a story, bad writers work backwards from outcome to cause, writing down things in the order they occur to them. Passive voice makes it easy. Using the passive voice unnecessarily can make it seem like the writer is trying to evade responsibility, even if they aren't (hint: as Pinker says, politicians love the passive voice!).

This is not a recommendation against using the passive voice. It depends on what one wants to draw the reader's attention to. When the attention needs to be on the "thing done", by all means, use the passive. Otherwise, use caution.

(iii) Using professional jargon

Classic style recommends that the writer assumes that the reader's intelligence is on par with that of the writer, not their state of knowledge. Overuse of professional jargon is a result of assuming that the reader's state of knowledge is on par with that of the writer. Often, this is not deliberate. Thinking back to the Calvin and Hobbes cartoon, some individuals may (and do) use jargon to protray themselves as knowledgeable, but the majority are not aware that this is what they are doing, especially when writing dissertations and journal articles. Overuse of jargon might seem like the writer is trying to obfuscate, even if they did not intend to.

One way to avoid this is to have a clear idea of who the intended audience is. For instance, the intended audience for a thesis is not its external examiner or the advisor, but a fellow student in the same field, though not necessarily the same subfield. Count on the reader to be a willing participant and maintain narrative structure.

(iv) Overuse of clichés

Clichés in academia take the following form:

  • Recent research indicates that...

  • The advent of new technologies has necessitated...

As William Safire says, avoid clichés like the plague!

(v) Signposting

Signposting is the practice of setting down signposts throughout an article. One says what one has to say, says it, and says what has just been said. This usually takes the form "The previous section explained concept 1. Now we will move on to explaining concept 2...".

Overuse of signposting interrupts the flow of reading. It is okay to use signposting in some places. For instance, at the beginning of a chapter in thesis or a journal article. If the article has long sections, minimal signposting at the beginning of those sections might help. In short, signposts must be introduced only where they can help the reader, not hinder them.

(vi) Hedging

Hedging is the practice of introducing words or phrases that allows the writer to remain ambiguous about their conclusions. Sometimes, the results of an experiment could genuinely be ambiguous and hedging is the correct thing to do in such situations, but it must be done carefully and be kept to a minimum.
